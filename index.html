Stokes' Law Observation Demo

Observing Terminal Velocity in a Viscous Fluid

General Description

This demonstration allows for the observation of an object falling through a high-viscosity (or "thick") fluid, such as corn syrup or glycerin. It provides a clear visualization of terminal velocity.

The setup consists of a tall, clear, sealed tube filled with the fluid. Small, dense spheres (like marbles or ball bearings) are sealed inside the fluid.

Instead of continuously accelerating, as an object would in a vacuum, the spheres are observed to do the following:

  • They quickly accelerate for a very short time.
  • They then settle into a slow, constant speed (terminal velocity) for the rest of their fall.
  • By using two spheres of the same material but different sizes, you can observe that the larger sphere falls with a faster terminal velocity than the smaller sphere.

How to Set Up the Observation

This setup is straightforward and focuses on clear observation of the falling spheres.

Materials:

  • A tall, clear, sealable straight-sided container or tube (a large test tube with a stopper, a clear plastic tube with end caps, or a sealed display tube is ideal).
  • A high-viscosity fluid (e.g., clear corn syrup, glycerin, or dish soap).
  • Two small, dense spheres of the same material but different sizes (e.g., a large and a small ball bearing, or a large and a small marble).
  • A ruler or tape measure.
  • A stopwatch.
  • Materials to create a waterproof seal (e.g., a tight-fitting stopper/cap, waterproof epoxy, or hot glue).

Instructions & Observation:

  1. Prepare the tube: Fill the tall container with your viscous fluid. Do not fill it completely; leave a small amount of air at the top to form a single bubble. This bubble is important for flipping the tube.
  2. Add the spheres: Gently drop the large and small spheres into the fluid.
  3. Seal the tube: Securely seal the tube with its cap or stopper. Use epoxy or another waterproof sealant to make it completely leak-proof. Allow the sealant to cure fully.
  4. Mark your distances: For a more quantitative observation, let the tube rest vertically. Once the spheres are at the bottom, use tape or a marker to make two lines on the *outside* of the container:
    • A "Start" line a few centimeters below the end (to allow the spheres to reach terminal velocity).
    • A "Finish" line near the other end (before the spheres get too close to the bottom).
    • Use the ruler to measure the exact distance between these two lines.
  5. Observe the fall: Hold the tube vertically until the spheres and air bubble are at opposite ends. Quickly invert (flip) the tube 180 degrees so the spheres are at the top and the air bubble is at the bottom (the bubble will immediately start to rise).
  6. Time the fall: Watch as the spheres fall through the fluid. Notice how they quickly settle into a constant speed. Use the stopwatch to time how long it takes *each* sphere to travel between the "Start" and "Finish" lines.
  7. Compare: You will have observed that both spheres reached a terminal velocity, and the larger sphere had a much shorter travel time (and thus a higher velocity) over the same distance.
  8. Repeat: To run the observation again, simply wait for the spheres to reach the bottom and then flip the tube over. You can repeat the measurement as many times as you like.
